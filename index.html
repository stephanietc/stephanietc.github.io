About Me

Hi! My name is Stephanie. I am a PhD student in Biomedical Engineering and the Collaborative Program in Neuroscience at the University of Toronto. I work at the Bloorview Research Institute, where I am also the Rounds Coordinator of the Bloorview Research Institute Trainee Executive.

In my doctoral work, I create and evaluate new interventions to improve motor control in children with cerebral palsy. Specifically, I am investigating the combined use of music-supported rehabilitation and video game therapy.

I am advised by Dr. Joyce Chen and Dr. Elaine Biddiss.

Current Project

Movement through Music

music-supported rehabilitation through video games

Cerebral palsy is the most common motor disorder in children. It often impacts upper limb movements required for activity and play. New home-based therapies are needed to target motor control with enough intensity and engagement for clinical outcomes.

I am developing MusicMaster, a video game therapy, to do just that. By pairing motion tracking technology with music-supported rehabilitation therapy techniques, MusicMaster may enhance motor learning in a fun, engaging way.

Past Projects

Sound Classification for Mixed-Reality Therapy Games

I used machine learning to detect and classify musical instruments from audio mixtures. In mixed-reality video games for motor therapy, these algorithms will complement motion tracking to detect and reward sounds associated with fine movements of music-making. Without my code, these movements may be missed by motion tracking alone.

Find out more

Physiological Models of Impaired Sound Localization

Sound localization is important for everyday listening. With Dr. Ian Bruce at McMaster University, I used computational models of the auditory brainstem and midbrain to see how sound localization cues are processed in impaired and aided hearing. For this work, which formed my MASc project, I was awarded a Certificate of Excellence for Outstanding Thesis.

Find out more

Biomusic: Listening to Anxiety

With Dr. Elaine Biddiss and Elizabeth Han, I evaluated an auditory interface to help caregivers detect anxiety in children with communication difficulties. We mapped physiological indicators of anxiety to sounds, creating lively "biomusic" that lets you hear anxious feelings in musical changes.
